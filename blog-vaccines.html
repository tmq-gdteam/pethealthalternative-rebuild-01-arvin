Vaccines

Can the Vaccines That Prevent Illness Also Endanger Your Dog?

By Dr. Cheryl A. Caputo Â· Pet Health Alternatives

Yes - vaccines that protect your dog from serious disease can, in some cases, also pose risks. The answer isn't to skip vaccines, but to avoid over-vaccinating. By testing immunity with a titer test and tailoring every shot to your dog's age, health, and lifestyle, you get the protection your dog needs without the unnecessary risk.

Is it safe to vaccinate your dog? Are common shots - such as the DHPP vaccine or the rabies vaccine - really that important? Even though dog vaccinations are easy to obtain, that doesn't mean your dog must automatically get every one of them. Over-vaccinating pets has become a genuine concern in the veterinary world, yet immunizing your dog remains an important part of responsible ownership. Over the last several years, many veterinarians have moved away from giving vaccinations annually, instead recommending parvo and distemper shots every three years.

Why vaccines aren't risk-free

Dog vaccinations are never entirely without potential risks, and that alone sparks debate. Some vaccines contain adjuvants - immune-system boosters that encourage the body to fight the disease the vaccine represents. These adjuvants can cause inflammation at the injection site. A number of vaccines also contain preservatives and mercury, a highly toxic chemical.

A dog's reaction to a vaccine can genuinely sap their strength, much like the body responding to a "real" illness. More specifically, a dog's white blood cell (WBC) count drops after vaccination. Logic tells us that for a period of time afterward, your dog's immunity is in a weakened state and they may be more susceptible to other diseases.

What to keep in mind when planning vaccinations

  • Vaccinations are important for puppies 7â€“14 weeks old, because this is when the immunity passed on from their mother begins to wane - leaving them more vulnerable to serious diseases.
  • Giving a distemper/parvovirus vaccination every three years is often unnecessary. Rather than vaccinate needlessly, it's better to test the level of immunity first with a titer test. Many dogs and cats turn out not to need vaccinating as often as every three years.
  • Autoimmune diseases have increased, and they can often be attributed to over-vaccination.
  • Vaccination programs should be custom-fit to your pet, based on their current health, lifestyle, and potential exposure to specific diseases.
  • Waivers are available for pets whose severe medical condition makes vaccination unsafe.

Watch for reactions

Report all symptoms to your vet immediately. If your dog has difficulty breathing or moving, develops hives, or shows swelling in the facial region, go to a veterinary clinic right away. If you're concerned, call your vet or an emergency clinic and ask whether you need to come in - and always know where the nearest clinic is, just in case.
